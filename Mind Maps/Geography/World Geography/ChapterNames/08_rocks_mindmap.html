🌋 1. Igneous Rocks
  • Formed by the cooling and solidification of lava or magma inside or outside the Earth
  • Examples: Granite, Basalt, Pegmatite, Dianite, Gabbro, Pumice, etc.

Intrusive (Inside the Earth)

  • Forms the Continental crust
  • Cools slowly under the surface (e.g., Granite)

Extrusive (Outside the Earth)

  • Forms the Oceanic crust
  • Cools rapidly on the surface (e.g., Basalt)
  • Weathering of basaltic Deccan Trap lava → Black (Regur) Soil

⚠ Exam Trap
Black (Regur) Soil comes from the weathering of Basalt — an Extrusive igneous rock — not from Intrusive rocks. This pairing is frequently mixed up in MCQs.
🏖️ 2. Sedimentary Rocks (Layered Rocks)
  • Formed by weathering and erosion of existing rocks
  • Process – Lithification
  • Examples: Sandstone, Limestone, Conglomerate, Rock salt, Shale, etc.
📌 Famous monuments Agra Fort & Red Fort of Delhi built using sedimentary rocks (sandstone)

⚡ Quick Recall
Both Sandstone → Quartzite and Shale → Slate — fine-grained sediments turn into hard, compact metamorphic rocks under pressure.
📂 Classification of Sedimentary Rocks

Mechanically / Inorganically Formed

  • Sand-dominant rocks (Sandstone)
  • Clay-dominant rocks (Shale)
  • Gravel-dominant rocks (Conglomerate)

Biologically / Organically Formed

  • Lime-dominant rocks (Limestone, Chalk)
  • Carbon-dominant rocks (Coal)

Chemically Formed

  • Rock Salt, Gypsum, Dolomite, Halite
  • Iron deposits, Stalactites
⚠ Exam Trap
Limestone is organically (biologically) formed from marine shells/calcium carbonate — don't classify it as clay-dominant/mechanical; Shale is the clay-dominant mechanical rock.
🔥 3. Metamorphic Rocks (Transformed Rocks)
  • Transformation of sedimentary and igneous rocks due to heat, pressure, and chemical reactions
  • Examples: Gneiss, Amphibolite, Greenstone, Serpentine, Phyllite, etc.

⚡ Quick Recall
Progressive metamorphism chain: Shale → Slate → Phyllite → Schist (increasing heat & pressure)

⚡ Quick Recall
All three rock types are interconvertible through the Rock Cycle — none is a permanent "final" form.

  • Weathering and Erosion drive the transition from Igneous → Sedimentary

⚠ Exam Trap
The Rock Cycle has no fixed starting point — any rock type can transform into any other given the right conditions; it is not a strictly one-directional sequence.
