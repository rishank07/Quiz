💧 Distribution of Water
  • Water covers 70.8% of the Earth's surface
CategoryDistribution

Fresh Water (2.5%)Snow/Glacier/Ice caps – 0.015% (68% of freshwater) • Groundwater – 0.58% (30%) • Fresh water in Lakes – 0.003% (2%) • Atmosphere – 0.0013% • Rivers – 0.0001%
🔄 Water Cycle
  • Conversion of water into vapour and vapour again into water
  • Key Processes: Evaporation, Transpiration, Condensation
  • Evaporation: Water → Water vapour (Liquid → Gas)
  • Condensation: Water vapour → Water
⚡ Quick Recall
Of all freshwater, Glaciers/Ice caps hold ~68% and Groundwater ~30% — together these two account for almost all usable freshwater; surface lakes and rivers are a tiny fraction.

🧂 Salinity of Ocean Water (Average – 35‰)
  • Amount of salt present per thousand grams of water (‰)
  • Average ocean salinity – 35 grams per thousand grams (35‰)
  • Salinity increases with increasing depth
  • Highest salinity band – Between 20°–40° North and 10°–30° South latitudes

🪸 Coral Reefs
  • Coral = Coral Polyps – formed by marine organisms
  • Symbiotic relationship with zooxanthellae algae
  • Lime-dominant structures – formed from Calcium Carbonate (CaCO₃)
  • Live in colonies in warm, shallow, clear saltwater
✅ Conditions Required for Coral Growth
  • Temperature – 20–21°C
  • Ocean depth till sunlight can penetrate
  • Sediment-free water
  • Normal marine salinity
  • Presence of Submarine platforms (relatively flat, shallow area in ocean)
📂 Types of Coral Reefs
TypeKey Details
1. Fringing Coral ReefSouth Florida, Islands of Malaysia and Solomon Islands, Gulf of Mannar, etc.
2. Barrier ReefWorld's largest – Great Barrier Reef – Pacific Ocean (Australia)
3. Coral Atoll / RingWorld-famous Funafuti Atoll, Atolls of Lakshadweep Island, etc.
🥀 Coral Bleaching
  • Effect of global warming
  • Density of zooxanthellae algae declines
  • Concentration of photosynthesis pigments within the zooxanthellae falls
⚡ Quick Recall
Great Barrier Reef (Australia) = World's largest Barrier Reef · Funafuti Atoll = World-famous Coral Atoll — two different reef types, two different records.
