🌡️ Air Pressure
  • The weight of an air column exerted per unit area
  • Pressure created by a mercury column 76 cm or 760 mm high
  • Unit: Millibar (mb) or Pascal (Pa)
  • Instrument: Barometer – weather indicator
  • Maximum air pressure: 1013.2 millibar (at sea level)
  • Isobar: A line connecting areas of equal atmospheric pressure
📊 Barometer Readings

Stormy

Rapidly falls in reading

Rain

Initially falls, then gradually rises

Anticyclone / Clear Weather

Continuous rise in reading

⚡ Quick Recall
A rapidly falling barometer reading warns of an incoming storm.
🌍 Pressure Belts (Latitudinal)
BeltLocation & Key Feature
Polar High Pressure90°N and 90°S – Cold, descending air
Subpolar Low Pressure60°–65°N and S – Rising air, cyclones
Subtropical High Pressure (Horse Latitudes)30°–35°N and S – Descending air, dry regions, calm winds
Equatorial Low Pressure (Doldrums)0°–5°N and S – Rising air, calm winds, heavy rainfall
⚠ Exam Trap
Both the Horse Latitudes (30–35°) and the Doldrums (0–5°) have calm winds, but for opposite reasons — Horse Latitudes = descending dry air; Doldrums = rising moist air. Don't mix them up.
🌬️ Winds
  • Horizontal air flow: High Pressure → Low Pressure (Pressure Gradient)
  • Warm air (low pressure) → Rises upward
  • Cold air (high pressure) → Sinks downward
🌀 Coriolis Force
  • Wind deflects – Right in Northern Hemisphere, Left in Southern Hemisphere (Ferrel's Law)
  • Magnitude: Zero at the Equator, Maximum at the Poles
  • Proportional to angle of latitude
📌 Geostrophic Winds Flow parallel to Isobars
⚡ Quick Recall
NHern → deflect Right · SHern → deflect Left (Ferrel's Law) — Coriolis Force is zero at the Equator.
🌦️ Inter Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ)
  • Zone where NE Trade Winds and SE Trade Winds converge
  • Shifts to 20°N–25°N latitude in July (Northern Hemisphere summer)
  • Forms a low pressure trough in equatorial latitudes
  • Region of calm winds (Doldrums), flanked by Northeastern and Southeastern Trade Winds
📌 Naming Winds Based on the direction they blow FROM (direction of origin)
⚡ Quick Recall
ITCZ's seasonal shift towards 20–25°N in July is a key driver of the Indian Monsoon.
🌐 1. Global / Permanent Winds (Prevailing, Constant, Planetary)

Trade Winds (Easterlies)

  • Converging winds
  • Flow from subtropical high pressure zones towards the equatorial low pressure belt

Westerlies

  • Flow from subtropical high pressure zones towards subpolar low pressure areas
  • Southern Hemisphere: 40° – Roaring Forties, 50° – Furious Fifties, 60° – Shrieking Sixties

Polar Winds

  • Flow from Polar High Pressure to Subpolar Low Pressure
  • Dry, cold, prevailing winds (Polar Easterlies)
📌 Horse Latitudes (30–35°N/S) Boundary separating Trade Winds and Westerlies
⚠ Exam Trap
The Southern Hemisphere Westerlies naming sequence — Roaring Forties (40°) → Furious Fifties (50°) → Shrieking Sixties (60°) — increases in intensity as latitude increases; a commonly tested sequence.
🍂 2. Seasonal Winds
  • Change in wind direction according to season and time
  • Monsoon Winds – seasonal change according to weather
🌅 Land Breeze and Sea Breeze

Sea Breeze (Day)

  • Flows from Sea → Land
  • Sea – High Pressure
  • Land – Low Pressure

Land Breeze (Night)

  • Flows from Land → Sea
  • Land – High Pressure
  • Sea – Low Pressure
📌 Land vs Water heating Land heats & cools quickly • Water heats & cools slowly
⚡ Quick Recall
Sea Breeze blows during the day (land is hotter, low pressure); Land Breeze blows at night (sea is warmer, low pressure).

🌪️ Jet Stream
  • Found in Upper Troposphere / Tropopause – 9–13 km
  • In winter, the subtropical westerly Jet Stream flows over the Western Indian region
  • This causes Western Disturbances – affecting Punjab, Haryana, Western Uttar Pradesh
