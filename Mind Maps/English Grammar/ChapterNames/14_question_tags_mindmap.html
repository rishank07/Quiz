QUESTION TAGS

English Grammar Mind Map · SSC / BPSC / BSSC

Definition

Question tags, also known as tag questions or question tails, are short phrases added to the end of a sentence to turn it into a question or to seek confirmation or agreement from the listener.

  • He is going to the party, isn't he?
  • He is not going to the party, is he?
The Basic Rule — Opposite Polarity
Positive Statement + Negative Question Tag
  • You are coming to the party, aren't you?
  • They have finished their homework, haven't they?
Negative Statement + Positive Question Tag
  • She isn't feeling well, is she?
  • John didn't eat lunch, did he?

I am writing a letter, aren't I?

⚡ QUICK RECALL The special irregular tag for "I am" is aren't I? — not "amn't I" or "am not I". This is the single most tested irregular tag form.

  • I am writing a letter, aren't I?
  • I am not writing a letter, am I?
Rule 1: No Helping Verb → Use Do/Does/Did
If a helping verb is NOT used in a sentence, use "do, does, did" in the question tag as needed
  • He writes a letter, doesn't he?
  • They write a letter, don't they?
  • He wrote a letter, didn't he?
⚡ QUICK RECALL Choose Do/Does/Did based on the main verb's tense in the statement: present simple → do/does; past simple → did — matching subject-verb agreement of the main sentence.
Rule 2: Nouns Not Used in Question Tags — Only Pronouns
Nouns are NOT used in the question tag — only pronouns are used
  • Rahul has completed the work, hasn't he?
  • Rakhi was dancing in the room, wasn't she?
⚠ EXAM TRAP Students often mistakenly repeat the proper noun in the tag ("Rahul has completed the work, hasn't Rahul?"). The tag must always use the matching pronoun, never the noun itself.
Rule 3: Negative Words → Positive Question Tag
Neither, No, Not, Never, Seldom, Nobody, Nothing, No one, None, Hardly, Rarely, Scarcely, Few, Little — these words make the sentence NEGATIVE, so the question tag is POSITIVE
  • They hardly talk to each other, do they?
  • She has seldom eaten chicken, has she?
  • A barking dog seldom bites, does it?
  • I know little about you, do I?
  • I have spent a little money, haven't I?
  • I know a few answers, don't I?
⚠ EXAM TRAP "A few" and "A little" have a POSITIVE meaning (unlike bare "few" and "little," which are negative) — so their question tags are NEGATIVE. This is the exact opposite of the main rule and a very common trap: compare "I know little about you, do I?" (negative "little" → positive tag) vs "I know a few answers, don't I?" (positive "a few" → negative tag).
Rule 4: Indefinite Pronoun Subjects → "They" in Tag
Everybody, Everyone, Somebody, Someone, Nobody, No one, Anybody, Anyone — as subject → use "they" in the question tag
  • Someone called me last night, didn't they?
  • Anyone can do this question, can't they?
  • Everybody loves me, don't they?
  • Nobody has seen God, have they?
⚡ QUICK RECALL These indefinite pronouns are grammatically singular for verb agreement in the main clause, but the question tag pronoun is always the plural "they" — a mismatch that's easy to forget under exam pressure.
Rule 5: Positive Imperative → Will you / Won't you
The question tag of a positive Imperative sentence is BOTH "will you" AND "won't you"
  • Switch off the lights, will you?
  • Switch off the lights, won't you?
  • Please help me, will you?
  • Please help me, won't you?
Rule 5 (Note): Negative Imperative → Only Will you
The question tag of a negative Imperative sentence is ONLY "will you"
  • Don't go there, will you?
  • Don't make a noise, will you?
⚠ EXAM TRAP Positive imperatives allow BOTH "will you" and "won't you" — but negative imperatives allow ONLY "will you", never "won't you". Using "won't you" after a negative imperative ("Don't go there, won't you?") is incorrect.
Rule 6: "Let's" → Shall we
If the Imperative sentence begins with "Let's," the question tag is "Shall we"
  • Let's party, shall we?
  • Let's play Holi, shall we?
Note: If the Imperative sentence begins with "Let him, Let her, Let them," the question tag is "Will you"
  • Let me talk to her, will you?
  • Let her dance, will you?
⚠ EXAM TRAP Don't confuse "Let's" (= let us, inviting shared action) with "Let him/her/them/me" (requesting permission from the listener for a third party or oneself). Only "Let's" specifically takes "shall we" — every other "Let + person" form takes "will you".
Rule 7: There/One/This-That/These-Those as Subject
If "there, one, this/that, these/those" are used AS THE SUBJECT of a sentence, use "there, one, it, they" respectively in the question tag
  • There is a pen on the table, isn't there?
  • One cannot solve this question, can one?
  • This is your bag, isn't it?
  • These are your books, aren't they?
Note: When Used as Adjectives Before Nouns
When "one, this/that, these/those" are used as ADJECTIVES before a noun, the question tag follows the number, person, and gender of that noun instead
  • One boy can solve this question, can't he?
  • This girl always loves me, doesn't she?
  • These bags are yours, aren't they?
⚠ EXAM TRAP Compare: "This is your bag, isn't it?" (this = subject/pronoun → "it") vs. "This girl always loves me, doesn't she?" (this = adjective before "girl" → tag matches "girl", i.e. "she"). Same word "this," completely different tag depending on its grammatical role.
Quantifier Subjects
  • All of us can solve this question, can't we?
  • Either of you is guilty, aren't you?
  • Neither of them is cool, are they?
  • Some of us are intelligent, aren't we?
  • None of them has seen London, have they?
⚡ QUICK RECALL With "Neither of..." and "None of...", the subject is treated as negative for the tag rule (positive tag follows), even though the main verb may appear singular ("is cool," "has seen") — the tag pronoun corresponds to "them"/"us"/"you" from the "of" phrase.
